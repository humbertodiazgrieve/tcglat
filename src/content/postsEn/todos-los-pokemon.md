# All Pokémon: The Complete National Pokédex Guide

Since Game Freak released the first Pokémon game in 1996, the franchise has become one of the most iconic in video game history. With more than three decades of trajectory, the Pokémon universe has grown to include more than a thousand unique creatures distributed across nine different generations. This complete guide will allow you to explore all Pokémon, understand the structure of the Pokédex, and discover the peculiarities of each region of the Pokémon world.

## How Many Pokémon Exist Currently in 2024

The answer to how many Pokémon there are in total is **1,025 Pokémon** as of 2024. This figure includes all creatures introduced from the first generation to the ninth, which corresponds to the Paldea region. However, it is important to note that this number can vary slightly depending on the alternate forms, regional variants, and special evolution forms that have been added over the years.

The number of Pokémon has grown exponentially from the 151 originals of the first generation. Each new installment of the saga has introduced approximately 100 to 150 new Pokémon, although the most recent ones have occasionally exceeded this figure. The ninth generation, with Pokémon Scarlet and Violet, added 120 new Pokémon to the collection, closing in style an admirable trajectory that continues to this day.

## The History of the National Pokédex

The National Pokédex is the official index that compiles all known Pokémon in the Pokémon world. Unlike regional Pokédexes, which only include Pokémon from a specific region, the National Pokédex covers the entirety of creatures discovered throughout history. This complete Pokédex has become the definitive resource for Trainers who wish to complete their collection.

The concept of the Pokédex arises in the original Pokémon games, where the player could register information about Pokémon encountered and caught. Over the years, the Pokédex has evolved significantly in functionality and design, going from simple text entries to complete databases with images, detailed statistics, habitats, and information about moves and evolutions. The National Pokédex has become an essential tool for any fan of the franchise.

### Evolution of the Pokédex Across Generations

In the first games, the Pokédex was a basic tool that registered caught Pokémon with limited information. Starting from the second generation, the ability to view detailed statistics, evolutions, and more complete descriptions was added. The third generation introduced categories and more detailed descriptions for each Pokémon. Since then, each new installment has improved the Pokédex experience, including it as an essential tool for any Trainer seeking to complete their Pokédex.

The most modern games have transformed the Pokédex into a complete interactive experience, allowing players to view three-dimensional models of Pokémon, listen to their characteristic sounds, explore their habitats, and obtain detailed information about their combat capabilities. This evolution has turned the Pokédex into much more than a simple catalog, transforming it into a comprehensive digital encyclopedia of the Pokémon universe.

## All Pokémon Generations: A Complete Tour

The generations of Pokémon represent each of the different main video game installments. Each generation introduces a new region, new Pokémon, and new game mechanics. Below, we present a detailed analysis of each generation, including the most iconic Pokémon of each one.

### First Generation: Kanto (Pokémon #1-151)

The first generation introduced the world of Pokémon with its legendary 151 original Pokémon, establishing the bases of a franchise that would revolutionize the entertainment industry. This region, inspired by the Kanto region in Japan, marked the beginning of an adventure that has captivated millions of fans around the world.

Pokémon number 1 is Bulbasaur, a Grass and Poison-type Pokémon that evolves into Ivysaur and later into Venusaur. Other iconic Pokémon of this generation include Pikachu (the most popular of the entire franchise), Charizard, Blastoise, Gyarados, Mewtwo, and many others. Pokémon names have been adapted for international markets, maintaining the essence of the original names while making them accessible to fans of different languages.

The first-generation Pokémon include creatures that have become globally recognized cultural symbols. Pokémon number 25, Pikachu, has transcended video games to become the official mascot of the entire franchise, appearing in movies, television series, merchandise, and even international events. The diversity of types introduced in this generation laid the foundation for the later expansions of the Pokémon universe.

The Kanto region presents a varied geography that ranges from bustling cities to dark caves and snowy mountains, offering a wide range of habitats for the 151 Pokémon of this generation. This variety of environments allowed for the creation of diverse and memorable Pokémon that continue to be fan favorites to this day.

### Second Generation: Johto (Pokémon #152-251)

The second generation doubled the number of available Pokémon, adding 100 new creatures to the collection. This expansion introduced mechanics that would become pillars of the franchise, including the breeding system and the day/night cycle that affects the behavior of certain Pokémon.

The Johto Pokémon include the iconic legendary Pokémon Lugia and Ho-Oh, considered the protectors of Johto, as well as fan-favorite Pokémon like Tyranitar, Blissey, Scizor, Ampharos, and many others. Pokémon number 152 in this generation is Chikorita, the Grass-type starter of the Johto region, following the tradition of starting each region with a Grass, Fire, or Water-type starter Pokémon.

The Johto region, inspired by the Kansai region in Japan, presented an improved evolution system and reintroduced the breeding system introduced in the first generation. The Pokémon of this generation are known for their creative designs and variety of types, including new types like Dark and Steel, which were added in this installment and significantly expanded the strategic possibilities in battle.

This generation also introduced the concept of additional evolutionary forms and Pokémon with two-stage evolutions with improved stats, paving the way for future expansions of the evolution system that would continue in the following generations.

### Third Generation: Hoenn (Pokémon #252-386)

With 135 new Pokémon, the third generation significantly expanded the Pokémon universe, introducing many of the mechanics that would define the franchise in the following years. This generation presented the ability system and natures, mechanics that added strategic depth to Pokémon combat.

The Hoenn Pokémon include the legendary Pokémon Groudon, Kyogre, and Rayquaza, which represent the elements of earth, sea, and sky respectively, as well as fan favorites like Blaziken, Swampert, Sableye, Latias, and many others. Pokémon number 252 is Treecko, the Grass-type starter of the Hoenn region, which continued the tradition of offering three starter Pokémon options.

The Hoenn region, inspired by the island of Kyushu in Japan, presented a wide variety of Pokémon with unique and creative designs. This generation also introduced the Dragon type and the Steel type, expanding the possibilities for type combinations and combat strategies. The Hoenn Pokémon include some of the most powerful evolutions of the entire series, with several Pokémon that continue to be relevant in official competitions to this day.

The third generation also introduced the concept of final evolutions for Pokémon that previously did not evolve beyond their second stage, adding depth to the evolution system and providing more options for Trainers seeking to complete their Pokédex.

### Fourth Generation: Sinnoh (Pokémon #387-493)

The fourth generation added 107 Pokémon to the National Pokédex, reintroducing Pokémon from previous generations with new evolutionary forms. This generation was notable for reintroducing evolutions for Pokémon that previously did not evolve, such as Magnemite, Electabuzz, and Munchlax.

The Sinnoh Pokémon include the legendary Pokémon Dialga, Palkia, and Giratina, related to time, space, and antimatter respectively, as well as popular Pokémon like Lucario, Garchomp, Togekiss, and many others. Pokémon number 387 is Turtwig, the Grass-type starter of the Sinnoh region, which introduced a new design for Grass-type starters.

The Sinnoh region, based on the island of Hokkaido in Japan, presented the introduction of the friendship evolution system and the final evolutions of Pokémon previously introduced in earlier generations. This generation also added new evolutionary forms for existing Pokémon, allowing Pokémon like Eevee, Snorlax, and others to adapt better to different combat strategies.

The Sinnoh Pokémon are known for their elegant designs and their connection to mythology, particularly the legendary Pokémon related to time and space, which have been central to the franchise's story.

### Fifth Generation: Unova (Pokémon #494-649)

The fifth generation added the largest number of Pokémon in a single installment: 156 new creatures. This expansion was notable not only for its size, but also for its focus on creating completely new Pokémon without direct inspiration from previous generations.

The Unova Pokémon include the legendary Pokémon Reshiram, Zekrom, and Kyurem, which represent the opposites of fire and electricity, as well as a wide variety of Pokémon with completely new designs. Pokémon number 494 is Victini, the starter Pokémon of this generation that combines elements of victory and region.

The Unova region, inspired by New York and its surroundings, presented a significant change in Pokémon design, moving toward a more realistic and detailed style. This generation also introduced the concept of dual and triple typing, expanding the possible combinations of types and combat strategies. The Unova Pokémon include some of the most innovative and distinctive designs of the series, like Zoroark, Chandelure, and Haxorus.

This generation introduced the concept of Pokémon with completely new designs without direct predecessors, allowing for greater creativity in the development of new creatures and expanding the possibilities of the Pokémon universe.

### Sixth Generation: Kalos (Pokémon #650-721)

The sixth generation introduced 72 new Pokémon, maintaining a more conservative number after the great expansion of the previous generation. This generation was notable for introducing the Fairy type, which added a new dimension to the type system.

The Kalos Pokémon include the legendary Pokémon Xerneas, Yveltal, and Zygarde, related to life, destruction, and ecological balance respectively, as well as popular Pokémon like Greninja, Aegislash, and Delphox. Pokémon number 650 is Chespin, the Grass-type starter of the Kalos region, which introduced a unique and distinctive design for starters.

The Kalos region, inspired by France, presented the introduction of the Fairy type and the Mega Evolution mechanic, which allows certain Pokémon to temporarily evolve during battle with increased stats and new types. This generation also introduced the individual stats system and formalized online competitive play, allowing players to compete internationally.

The Kalos Pokémon stand out for their elegant and sophisticated designs, reflecting the French inspiration of the region. This generation also introduced the concept of alternate forms for existing Pokémon through Mega Evolutions, expanding the strategic options available to Trainers.

### Seventh Generation: Alola (Pokémon #722-809)

The seventh generation added 88 Pokémon, including regional forms of existing Pokémon that adapted to the different ecosystems of the Alola islands. This generation introduced a revolutionary concept that allowed existing Pokémon to have different appearances and types depending on the region.

The Alola Pokémon include the legendary Pokémon Solgaleo, Lunala, and Necrozma, related to the sun, moon, and light respectively, as well as popular Pokémon like Primarina, Incineroar, and Rowlet. Pokémon number 722 is Rowlet, the Grass-type starter of the Alola region.

The Alola region, inspired by Hawaii, introduced the concept of regional forms, variations of existing Pokémon adapted to the different ecosystems of the region's four islands. For example, Alolan Vulpix is Ice type instead of Fire, while Alolan Sandshrew is Steel and Ice type instead of Ground. This innovation allowed classic Pokémon to be reinterpreted in completely new ways.

This generation also presented Z-Moves and the Ultra Burst mechanic, mechanics that added new strategic possibilities and allowed Pokémon to surpass their limits during battle.

### Eighth Generation: Galar (Pokémon #810-905)

The eighth generation introduced 96 new Pokémon, including the Galar region inspired by the United Kingdom. This generation presented significant innovations like Gigantamax, a temporary evolution that increases the size of Pokémon and changes their appearance.

The Galar Pokémon include the legendary Pokémon Zacian, Zamazenta, and Eternatus, related to the sword and shield, as well as popular Pokémon like Rillaboom, Cinderace, and Inteleon. Pokémon number 810 is Grookey, the Grass-type starter of the Galar region.

This generation presented Gigantamax and DLCs that expanded the Galar region with the Isle of Armor and Crown Tundra areas, adding additional Pokémon like Calyrex and the Crown Tundra legendaries. The Galar Pokémon represent a fresh approach to design, combining traditional franchise elements with a modern and distinctive style.

The eighth generation also introduced the possibility of transferring Pokémon from previous games through Pokémon HOME, allowing Trainers to maintain their collections across different generations of games.

### Ninth Generation: Paldea (Pokémon #906-1025)

The ninth and most recent generation added 120 new Pokémon, the largest amount since the fifth generation. This generation represents the current frontier of the Pokémon universe, introducing new mechanics and a revolutionary open world.

The Paldea Pokémon include the legendary Pokémon Koraidon and Miraidon, related to the past and future respectively, as well as popular Pokémon like Sprigatito, Fuecoco, and Quaxly. Pokémon number 906 is Sprigatito, the Grass-type starter of the Paldea region.

The Paldea region, inspired by the Iberian Peninsula, introduced the Terastal system, which allows any Pokémon to change its type during battle according to the type of crystal it uses. This generation also presented the open world characteristic of Pokémon Scarlet and Violet, allowing players to freely explore a vast and diverse region.

The Paldea Pokémon include designs that reflect the cultural and geographic diversity of Spain and Portugal, with Pokémon ranging from Mediterranean creatures to dragons of Iberian tradition. This generation also introduces new evolution forms and mechanics that continue to evolve the franchise.

## The Complete Pokédex: Structure and Functionality

The National Pokédex organizes all Pokémon in sequential numerical order, starting with Pokémon number 1 (Bulbasaur) and currently ending at Pokémon number 1025. This system allows Trainers to quickly locate any Pokémon using its unique Pokédex number.

The Pokédex system has evolved to become a more interactive and complete tool. Modern games allow players to view three-dimensional models of Pokémon, listen to their characteristic voices, and obtain information about their combat capabilities. This evolution has turned the Pokédex into a complete digital encyclopedia of the Pokémon universe.

### How the Pokédex Works

The Pokédex stores detailed information about each Pokémon, including its name, types, height, weight, description, location, base stats, possible moves, and evolutions. In the most recent games, the Pokédex also includes information about the habitats where each Pokémon can be found and the methods to obtain them, whether by evolution, capture in the wild, or trade.

The different types of Pokédex have specific purposes: the Kanto Pokédex organizes Pokémon by national number, while regional Pokédexes allow Trainers to locate specific Pokémon from each region. This organization allows fans to access detailed information about any Pokémon in the list of Pokémon.

## All Pokémon Generations: Key Statistics

Each generation has contributed uniquely to the expansion of the Pokémon universe, adding not only new Pokémon but also new mechanics and features that have defined the franchise:

- **First generation**: Established the fundamentals with 151 original Pokémon and the basic types, creating the bases of the combat system that persists to this day
- **Second generation**: Doubled the content and introduced the Dark and Steel types, adding depth to the type system
- **Third generation**: Expanded the combat system with abilities and natures, allowing greater customization of Pokémon
- **Fourth generation**: Reintroduced evolutions for previously introduced Pokémon and added new evolutionary forms
- **Fifth generation**: The largest expansion with 156 unique new Pokémon, maintaining the tradition of innovation
- **Sixth generation**: Introduced the Fairy type and Mega Evolutions, transforming competitive play
- **Seventh generation**: Presented regional forms and Z-Moves, offering new interpretations of classic Pokémon
- **Eighth generation**: Introduced Gigantamax and DLCs, expanding playable content
- **Ninth generation**: The Terastal system and the open world, taking the franchise to new dimensions
